Screen 1 · Overview
Payment Plan Modal
Merchant checkout → "Pay with Zip" modal

Payment Plan Selector

Context

Customer is at a merchant's checkout (e.g. Best Buy) and taps "Pay with Zip." This modal opens showing the available payment plans before they commit to a plan and continue checkout.

Screen 1 · Pitfalls
Top 5
What's working against conversion

Pitfalls.

1
No visible primary CTA
Each plan row is the entire tap target — there's no "Select" button, so first-time users may not realize tapping the row commits them.
High severity
2
Both plans expanded by default
Full amortization schedules for both options show at once, pushing trust badges and terms far down before any reassurance content appears.
High severity
3
No order context
Merchant name, item, and purchase total never appear in the modal — only payment breakdowns, leaving no reassurance the amount is correct.
Medium severity
4
35% APR sits in 10px type next to a 0-APR-looking option
Once noticed, this can trigger a trust/skepticism spike on the 8-pay plan relative to the 4-pay plan.
Medium severity
5
Binary exit only
The only way out is the full-close "X" — no lower-friction "back to checkout," so hesitation likely shows up as full abandonment.
Low severity
Screen 1 · Opportunities
Impact vs. effort
Where to focus next

Opportunities.

1
Add a visible "Select" affordance on each plan row
Pure discoverability fix — doesn't change layout structure.
Impact: HighEffort: Low
2
Default to a single expanded plan, collapse the other
Surfaces trust badges sooner and reduces initial scroll depth.
Impact: HighEffort: Medium
3
Add a "recommended" / "lowest cost" badge on one plan
Classic anchor to cut comparison paralysis between the two plans.
Impact: MediumEffort: Low
4
Show merchant name + order total inside the modal header
Closes the trust gap — needs order data passed through from checkout context.
Impact: MediumEffort: Medium
5
Add a soft secondary exit ("Back to checkout")
Distinct from the hard close — likely cheap to test, could reduce abandonment at the "X."
Impact: MediumEffort: Low

Online Virtual Card & In-Store Card

Context

Full "Spending Power" screen has many components (balance, CLI tracker, banner, info links), but the audit focus is the 2 card rows up top — Online virtual card and In-store card — since those are the key items in the purchase funnel.

Top 5
What's working against conversion

Medium severity

Medium severity

Medium severity
4
Icon-only chevron affordance

Low severity

Medium severity

Impact: MediumEffort: Low

e.g. "Activate to use in-store" — turns a dead end into a next step.

Screen 2 · Bigger Picture
Beyond the 2 cards
Entry point: tapping "Spending Power" from home

It answers "how much."
It doesn't answer "for what."

A customer taps into Spending Power because they want to know what they can actually do right now — afford a specific item, decide whether to check out, or just see what's possible. Today the screen mostly hands back an abstract number, two card balances, and a future-oriented growth mechanic (CLI tracker). None of it closes the loop back to "so what can I buy."

1
No "where can I use this" path
The power number never links out to eligible merchants, categories, or a "start shopping" action — it's informational, not a jumping-off point.
Impact: HighEffort: High
2
No entry-context awareness
If a customer arrived mid-purchase to check affordability, the screen should say "yes, you can afford this" and route back — instead everyone sees the same static dashboard.
Impact: HighEffort: Medium
3
The one concrete offer is buried
"You may be approved to Pay in 4 up to $640..." is the only moment that turns the number into something specific — but it's sandwiched between the cards and the CLI tracker, easy to skip past.
Impact: MediumEffort: Low
4
No scaled suggestions
Nothing shows what's actually purchasable at ~$567 (merchants, categories, price ranges) — the number stays abstract instead of becoming a shopping starting point.
Impact: MediumEffort: High

Screen 4 · Overview
Merchant Shopping
Search → Amazon autofill → in-app merchant site

Shop Amazon → Spend Amount

Context

User searches "Amazon," taps the autofill result, and lands on Amazon's site inside the Zip app. The moment they arrive, Frame 1 ("Shopping at Amazon") appears. Tapping "Shop [Amazon]" leads to Frame 2, asking how much they want to spend. Tapping "Set up your virtual card" in Frame 1 takes them elsewhere.

Flagged up front

(1) User doesn't know what they'll shop for yet. (2) Feels like the wrong time to ask for a spend amount. (3) Setting up the virtual card right now, at first arrival, may pull users away from their actual goal before they even know what they want.

Screen 4 · Pitfalls
Top 5
What's working against conversion

Pitfalls.

1
"How much would you like to spend" arrives before intent exists
The amount input follows immediately after landing on Amazon's homepage — before the user has browsed or picked anything to buy.
High severity
2
Hard modal blocks the very first moment on the merchant site
A 50%-dark overlay + bottom sheet forces a decision before the user has seen anything on Amazon yet.
High severity
3
"Set up your virtual card" competes for attention at the worst moment
Offering card setup right at arrival — before the user knows what they want — adds a detour on top of an already premature ask.
Medium severity
4
No visible "just browse first" path
Only two labeled options exist (Shop / Set up card) — no explicit way to say "not yet, let me look around" without relying on an unlabeled swipe-down.
Medium severity
5
Order-total input ignores visible spending power
$647 spending power is visible in the dimmed background, but the input starts blank with no suggested amount — full guesswork lands on the user.
Medium severity
Screen 4 · Opportunities
Impact vs. effort
Where to focus next

Opportunities.

1
Replace the hard modal with a lightweight, dismissible entry point
e.g. a collapsed banner/pill ("Tap when ready to checkout with Zip") instead of a full dimmed takeover — lets browsing happen uninterrupted.
Impact: HighEffort: Medium
2
Move the amount ask to when real intent exists
Trigger it at cart/checkout on the merchant site rather than on arrival, when there's an actual order total to work from.
Impact: HighEffort: High
3
Pre-fill the amount using visible spending power
Suggest/default toward the $647 already shown instead of a blank input, cutting the guesswork.
Impact: MediumEffort: Low
4
Add an explicit "I'll decide later" dismissal
A labeled way out, not just an implicit swipe-down, so users aren't forced into a binary choice before they're ready.
Impact: MediumEffort: Low

Push "Set up your virtual card" to a later, checkout-adjacent point instead of competing for attention at arrival.

Screen 5 · Overview
Fallback Active PR Modal
Proactive reconciliation, not merchant navigation

Did You Complete This Purchase?

Context

Triggered when a customer has an active Purchase Request (PR) but wasn't just navigating a merchant — Zip proactively checks in. "Yes, I completed this purchase" sets the PR to 'pending' and hides it, then returns the user to the merchant webview — now in its "no active VC" state, with a persistent "Pay with Zip" button in the bottom toolbar. "No, I never spent it" triggers the second frame, which resurfaces the virtual card webview.

Screen 5 · After "Yes"
Focus: the "Pay with Zip" button
Where "Yes, I completed this purchase" leads

Pay with Zip — Persistent Toolbar

Context

After confirming "Yes," the PR closes and the merchant webview reverts to its no-active-card state — a persistent bottom toolbar with a "Pay with Zip" button, inviting a new purchase during the same browsing session. Analysis focus is this button and what happens after tapping it. The ESP banner above it is out of scope here — already covered in Screen 2.

Screen 5 · Pitfalls
Top 4
What's working against conversion

A mostly well-designed check-in.

1
"Pay with Zip" re-triggers the same premature amount-ask flow flagged in Screen 4
Tapping it almost certainly opens the Shop → spend-amount flow again — before any new shopping intent exists, same root issue as before, just a second entry point into it.
High severity
2
"No, I never spent it" resurfaces the card with no reorientation
User lands back on a blank webview + card sheet with no copy explaining why they're seeing it again or what to do next (e.g. "head back to Amazon to finish up").
Medium severity
3
"Pay with Zip" label doesn't match what happens next
Reads like an immediate payment action, but the next step is filling in a budget/amount — not a charge. The label sets the wrong expectation.
Medium severity
4
No closing acknowledgment after "Yes, I completed this purchase"
The interaction resolves silently — a one-line confirmation would close the loop.
Low severity
✓
The core mechanic itself is a good pattern
Recognizing an ambiguous system state (unclear if a PR converted) and proactively resolving it via a direct question — rather than leaving it stuck — is the right move. Showing merchant, date, and amount gives exactly enough to jog memory, and "Some merchants like Amazon can take up to a few days to charge a completed purchase" pre-empts confusion about the delay. Equal visual weight on both buttons is also correct here — it's a neutral factual question, not a decision that should be nudged one way.

Impact: HighEffort: Low
2
Add reorientation copy when the card resurfaces
e.g. "Ready when you need it — head back to Amazon to complete your purchase" instead of a bare card sheet on a blank page.
Impact: MediumEffort: Low

Impact: MediumEffort: Low

Screen 6 · Overview
Online PR Flow
After the amount is set on a Purchase Request

How Would You Like to Pay?

Context

After a customer sets the amount on a PR, they choose how to pay it. The question driving this audit isn't just function/interaction — is this the best available approach? What's missing? Should it be more illustrative, more descriptive, reflect the actual purchase, be more brandable? Does it give users a sense of ease and continuity in choosing their plan?

Screen 6 · Pitfalls
Top 4
What's working against conversion

Solid bones, thin surface.

1
Inconsistent fee transparency between options
The 2-payment row says "Lower fees" outright, but the pre-selected 4-payment default shows no cost info at all — a user could commit to it without ever seeing what it costs, unless they tap "Learn more."
High severity
2
No purchase context beyond a dollar figure
"Order total $40.07" is shown, but nothing reminds the user what they're actually buying or where — the decision feels detached from the purchase itself.
Medium severity
3
Zero illustrative cue for payment cadence
"Pay biweekly over 6 weeks" is pure text — no lightweight visual (e.g. a small dot-timeline) reinforces how the payments actually land over time.
Medium severity
4
No brand presence at a financial-commitment moment
Fully utilitarian — no Zip personality or reassurance styling right when the user is making a real money decision. Debatable: minimalism has a case here too, but it's worth a deliberate choice rather than a default.
Low severity
✓

One pre-selected default plus a single visible "Continue" fixes Screen 1's two biggest pitfalls (no default, no visible CTA).
